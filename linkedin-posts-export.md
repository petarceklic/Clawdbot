Australia just spent $64 million on a bus ticketing app that still doesn't fully work.

The parliamentary inquiry called it "clearly not ready for launch." They launched it anyway.

Day one: QR codes didn't scan. Cards failed. Validators broke. The app crashed. The Transport Minister got censured.

Sound familiar? A few months earlier, the Bureau of Meteorology spent $96.5 million on a weather website so bad they had to bring the old one back.

The technology to build these things exists. It's not the hard part.

The hard part is making sure someone in the room has the authority to say "this isn't ready for real people yet." And in both cases, nobody did.

This isn't a tech problem. It's a design problem disguised as a tech problem.

If you don't have someone obsessed with how people actually use the thing, you're not building a product. You're building a press release.

Every major public rollout should have a red team whose only job is to break the experience before the public does.

The cost of not testing is always higher than the cost of testing. Always.

Ferrari's first electric car is here.

And it looks like an Apple Store inside.

Jony Ive designed the interior. Machined aluminium. Gorilla glass everywhere. Mechanical dials. Minimal everything.

It's beautiful. I'm not arguing that.

But it doesn't look like Ferrari.

Strip the badge off the steering wheel and you'd never guess the brand. That's a design problem.

Ive has the philosophy right. Physical controls over touchscreens. Tactile over digital. I'm behind that.

But when you hire the most famous designer in the world, you get his design language. Not Ferrari's.

The best design work disappears into the brand it serves. This feels like it arrived with a signature already on it.

Ferrari hired Jony Ive to design a Ferrari. He designed a Jony Ive.

Panels is a classic example of designing something delightful in a space that does not have a real problem.
As product designers, we talk a lot about friction, jobs to be done, and behaviour change. Panels had almost no leverage on any of those.

Officially, the team said the numbers just did not work. Big downloads, but low retention, high content costs, and no habit loop to bring people back. Wallpaper swapping is free, quick, and something people barely think about, so the business never sustained itself.

For me, the takeaway is simple. Good design cannot compensate for a weak problem space.
Before you craft beautiful UI, make sure the behaviour you are improving is actually worth improving.

#productdesign #uxdesign #creatoreconomy

Wild how nobody talks about the real killer of productivity: context switching.

You can be great at what you do, but if your day is chopped into different clients and shifting priorities, your focus never gets a clean run. Studies show it can take more than twenty minutes to properly recover after each switch, which explains why the work feels heavier.

Agencies run on that pace and sometimes that's unavoidable. But for me, shifting to retainers and giving each day to one client changed everything. Deep focus became normal again, and the work felt clearer and faster because I wasn't rebuilding context every hour.

Calmer mind, higher quality, happier clients.

#productivity #designlife #deepwork

## Post #9 — December 2025

Hot take: design subscriptions sound great until you actually run one.

I tried it for a year. Clients loved the idea of "unlimited" design. I loved the idea of predictable income. Everyone wins, right?

Not quite.

It quickly turned into a race. Fast briefs. Faster turnarounds. No time to think deeply or solve the real problem. Just constant requests and notifications.

Design isn't a drive-through service. It's problem solving. It needs context, patience, and conversation.

What has worked far better is something far less exciting, a simple retainer. It's calm, consistent, and it gives clients proper results instead of quick fixes.

It's not trendy, but it works. And that's what actually matters.

Everyone's losing it over NEO, the new humanoid robot that supposedly folds laundry, vacuums, waters plants, and makes dinner while you're at work.

Except, most of the demo wasn't "AI" at all. It was remote controlled by a human in another room.

This is where product design and AI ambition collide. The rush to show something magical before it's truly autonomous risks breaking trust. The dream is real, but you can't sell the dream before you've built the capability.

Being first to market only matters if your product can stand on its own. Otherwise, you're just teaching users to be sceptical of the next announcement.

The secret to a full calendar isn't better pixels, it's better business.

Most freelance designers double down on craft. But the ones who stay fully booked know how to set clear expectations, understand their client's commercial goals, and communicate in outcomes, not aesthetics.
